Water-Cooled SMRs

Established light-water technology maintained in compact, integral designs.

Coolant & Moderator

Water-cooled SMR-PWRs operate on established light-water technology, using water as both a coolant and moderator within a compact, integral reactor vessel. By utilizing natural circulation driven by density differences, these designs eliminate the need for reactor coolant pumps, significantly reducing parasitic power consumption and mechanical failure modes.

Fuel & Core Design

The core utilizes standard UO2 fuel assemblies (enrichment < 5% U-235). Its compact geometry supports natural circulation and maintains strongly negative reactivity feedback. The entire primary system is enclosed in a compact steel containment, managed through control rods and burnable absorbers.

Safety Features

  • Control rods insert by gravity during shutdown.
  • Passive heat exchangers remove decay heat.
  • Elimination of large primary piping reduces LOCA probability.

Ensures cooling without operator action or external power.

Refueling & Waste

Cycles range between 18–24 months with burnups exceeding 45 GWd/tHM. Spent fuel is stored in pools and then dry casks (MPC-37). Integral designs reduce neutron leakage, slowing vessel embrittlement and limiting structural activation.

Technical Analysis

Advantages

  • Well-understood technology: Decades of operational data
  • Strong neutron moderation efficiency: Higher neutron moderation
  • Easier regulatory approval: Easier Licensing, faster deployment

Disadvantages

  • Corrosion challenges: High temperature and pressure causes Stress corrosion cracking
  • High-pressure operation (~15 MPa): Potential for rapid depressurization in accidents
  • Neutronic & thermal-hydraulic challenges: Compact core → higher neutron leakage & power density

Gas-Cooled SMRs

High-temperature helium cooling with TRISO fuel for efficient process heat.

Coolant & Moderator

SMR-GCRs utilize chemically inert Helium gas as a coolant, exiting the core at temperatures between 700–950°C. High-purity Graphite serves as both the moderator and reflector, offering extreme temperature tolerance (sublimation at 3600°C) and massive thermal buffering.

TRISO Fuel Particles

The core uses TRISO particles—miniature containment units with UO₂ or UCO kernels wrapped in ceramic layers (SiC). These retain fission products above 1600°C. Fuel is used in either mobile pebbles (online refueling) or stationary prismatic blocks.

Inherent Safety

  • RCCS: The Reactor Cavity Cooling System removes heat via natural convection and radiation.
  • Negative Feedback: Physics-based reduction of power during overheating.
  • Helium Safety: Eliminates boiling, chemical reactions, or hydrogen risks.

Refueling & Cycle

These reactors reach high thermal efficiencies (~47%). Pebble-bed designs support online refueling, while prismatic reactors follow 18–36 month cycles. Spent TRISO fuel is transferred directly to dry storage, requiring no prolonged pool cooling.

Technical Analysis

Advantages

  • High thermal efficiency and outlet temperature
  • Chemically inert coolant: no material corrosion
  • Strong accident tolerance: Due to TRISO fuel particles
  • Excellent neutron economy

Disadvantages

  • Graphite irradiation: Induced dimensional changes.
  • Complex and costly manufacturing: TRISO fuel fabrication
  • Helium: Leakage risks, requires high pressure operation and high circulation power
  • Material challenges due to high temperatures

Liquid Metal SMRs

Highly efficient fast reactors using Sodium, Lead, or LBE coolants.

Coolant Selection

LM-SMRs use Sodium (Na), Lead (Pb), or Lead-Bismuth Eutectic (LBE) to achieve high thermal efficiency and low-pressure operation. These metals offer thermal conductivity significantly higher than water, enabling rapid heat extraction.

Fast Reactor Core

LM-SMRs typically operate as fast neutron reactors (0.1–1 MeV) without moderators. Fuel assemblies are arranged in hexagonal lattices using UO2 (15–20% enrichment), MOX, or metallic fuels (U-Zr). Metallic fuels offer superior conductivity and breeding potential, reaching burnups of 80–100 GWd/t.

Safety Mechanisms

  • Passive Decay Heat: Natural circulation removes up to 10% of nominal power without pumps.
  • Inherent Feedback: Relies on Doppler broadening and coolant-density effects.
  • Atmospheric Pressure: Operates at 0.1–0.2 MPa, minimizing mechanical stress.

Durability & Control

Lead and LBE require strict oxygen concentration control to prevent steel corrosion. Sodium systems utilize double-walled piping and inert cover gas to manage chemical reactivity with air and water. Cladding uses advanced HT-9 ferritic–martensitic steel.

Technical Analysis

Advantages

  • Fast neutron spectrum: High neutron economy
  • Low-pressure operation at high temperature: Eliminates LOCA
  • Superior heat transfer properties: Very high thermal conductivity and high volumetric heat capacity
  • Actinide burning capability

Disadvantages

  • Materials & Corrosion Issues
  • Radioactive Polonium (LBE systems)
  • High Coolant Density (Pb/LBE)
  • Maintenance & Additional Design Complexity

Molten Salt SMRs

Innovative designs using molten salts as coolant and potentially fuel medium.

Coolant & Moderator

MSRs use molten halide salts as primary coolant, operating at high temperatures (600–900°C) while maintaining near-atmospheric pressure. This eliminates high-pressure accident risks. These salts are chemically stable and provide strong negative temperature feedback. Only MSRs allow for liquid fuel, enabling online reprocessing.

Fuel & Core Design

Fuel can be liquid (dissolved in salt) or solid (TRISO particles). Liquid-fueled designs allow for online refueling and continuous removal of gaseous fission products, suppressing xenon poisoning. Heat is transferred via three loops: a radioactive primary salt loop, a clean intermediate salt loop, and a tertiary power conversion loop.

Safety Features

  • Freeze Plug: A salt section that melts during overheating, draining fuel by gravity into subcritical tanks.
  • Low Pressure: Operates at atmospheric pressure, reducing structural mechanical stress.
  • Passive Cooling: Decay heat is removed via natural convection and radiation.

Operation & Transients

MSRs exhibit self-regulating behavior due to strong negative reactivity coefficients. Continuous stripping of gases like Xenon and Krypton maintains stable reactivity. However, salt chemistry requires precise control to manage corrosion and prevent salt freezing in compact configurations.

Technical Analysis

Advantages

  • Stable, non-flammable molten salts prevent hydrogen explosions and sodium fires.
  • Liquid fuel resists radiation and mechanical damage, improving core lifetime
  • High thermal inertia enhances decay heat removal after shutdown
  • Excellent neutron economy and flexible fuel cycle.

Disadvantages

  • Corrosion risks and difficult salt chemistry control
  • Tritium generation requiring advanced containment
  • Complex fuel processing and higher infrastructure costs
  • Uncertainty in long-term salt stability and waste disposal
  • Limited commercial experience and regulatory challenges
