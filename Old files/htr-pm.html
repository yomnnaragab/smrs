HTR-PM

Case Study II • China

Overview

China’s high-temperature gas-cooled reactor program began with the HTR-10, a 10 MWₜ pebble-bed test reactor built at Tsinghua University and brought to full power in 2003. Extensive experiments on HTR-10 demonstrated the inherent safety characteristics of modular HTGRs under challenging operating conditions. The success of this program led to the launch of the HTR-PM pebble-bed reactor project in 2001, marking the transition from experimental validation to a larger, modular reactor design.

Target Applications

1. Electricity Generation
2. District Heating
3. Industrial Steam Supply
4. Hydrogen Production

Design Philosophy

The HTR-PM design prioritizes inherent and passive safety through TRISO fuel, a low-power-density pebble-bed core, and helium coolant, allowing safe operation without active systems or operator intervention, even in severe accidents. Its modular configuration improves simplicity, flexibility, and scalability, while graphite moderation and high thermal inertia keep temperatures below fuel failure limits during cooling or flow losses. Emphasis on standardized, manufacturable components further enhances reliability and industrial practicality.

Safety Features

The HTR-PM achieves a high level of inherent safety through passive mechanisms, robust physical design, and multiple engineered barriers. It features a low radioactive inventory, strong accident tolerance, and inherent safety characteristics such as low power density, high-quality TRISO fuel, and a large negative temperature coefficient. Decay heat is removed passively through conduction, radiation, and the Reactor Cavity Cooling System, ensuring fuel temperatures remain within safe limits even without active systems. Multiple containment barriers and effective chemical control measures further limit radioactive release and mitigate water or air ingress, ensuring safe operation under both normal and accident conditions.

Fuel Cycle Approach

The HTR-PM adopts a once-through pebble-bed fuel cycle using spherical TRISO-coated fuel elements containing UO₂ kernels. Fuel is handled and stored in dedicated transport and storage systems that maintain subcritical conditions and support continuous operation. Unlike conventional reactors, HTR-PM uses fully automated on-power refueling, with fresh pebbles added at the top of the core and spent or partially burned pebbles discharged from the bottom, measured for burnup, and either recycled or removed. The fuel cycle progresses from an initial mixed graphite–fuel core to equilibrium operation with fully enriched fuel, enabling stable power production and efficient core management without shutdown refueling.

Waste Management

The HTR-PM waste management strategy uses automated handling and long-term on-site dry storage for spent fuel, with tanks transferred through staged storage areas to allow decay heat reduction. Spent fuel is stored in sealed, shielded tanks with passive cooling ensured by natural ventilation, even under accident conditions. Final disposal is planned in a national geological repository
